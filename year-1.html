First Year Economics Curriculum

Your guide to the foundational courses in the first-year economics honors syllabus, covering economic theory and mathematical methods. This critical first year is designed to build the essential toolkit for all future economic study. You will be introduced to the fundamental principles that govern both individual decision-making (Microeconomics) and the economy as a whole (Macroeconomics). Simultaneously, you will develop a rigorous foundation in the mathematical techniques required to model, analyze, and understand complex economic theories, ensuring you are prepared for the quantitative demands of the rest of the degree.

Semester I

Core Course 1: Introductory Microeconomics

This course introduces the fundamental principles of microeconomics, a key component of the first-year economics syllabus. It covers basic concepts of supply and demand, consumer theory, production and costs, and market structures. The goal is to build a strong foundation for understanding how individual agents make decisions and interact in markets.

Key Topics:

1. The Scope and Method of Economics

This topic defines economics as the study of scarcity, choice, and opportunity cost. It introduces the methods economists use, distinguishing between positive analysis (what is) and normative analysis (what ought to be).

2. Supply and Demand: How Markets Work

This is the foundational model of market economics. You'll learn how the interaction of buyers (demand) and sellers (supply) determines prices and quantities, and how government policies like taxes or price controls can affect market outcomes.

3. Consumer Behavior and Utility Maximization

This unit explores how consumers make choices. It introduces the concepts of utility (satisfaction), budget constraints (what consumers can afford), and how consumers maximize their utility subject to their budget.

4. The Firm: Production and Costs

Here, the focus shifts to the producers. You will learn about the production function, which relates inputs to outputs, and the different types of costs firms face in the short run and long run (e.g., fixed, variable, marginal costs).

5. Perfect Competition and Monopoly

This topic introduces the two extreme forms of market structure. You'll analyze how perfectly competitive firms (price-takers) and monopolies (single sellers) decide their output levels and prices to maximize profit.

Core Course 2: Mathematical Methods for Economics-I

This course equips students with the essential mathematical tools required for formal economic analysis. It focuses on preliminary concepts, functions of one variable, and their applications in economics. The emphasis is on developing the skills to translate economic problems into mathematical models, a crucial skill for any economics student.

Key Topics:

1. Logic, Sets, and Number Systems

This unit builds the absolute foundation for mathematical reasoning. It covers the basics of logical proofs, set theory (unions, intersections), and the properties of real numbers that underpin all economic models.

2. Functions of One Real Variable

This topic introduces the core concept of a function (e.g., a demand function or a cost function). You will study different types of functions (linear, quadratic, exponential) and their graphical representations.

3. Single-Variable Differentiation

This is the heart of "marginal" analysis in economics. You will learn the rules of differentiation (finding the derivative) to measure the rate of change, such as finding marginal cost from a total cost function.

4. Applications of Derivatives in Economics

This unit applies the tool of differentiation to solve economic problems. This includes finding profit-maximizing output levels (where marginal revenue equals marginal cost) and calculating elasticities.

5. Sequences, Series, and Limits

This topic introduces the concept of convergence and limits, which is fundamental to understanding economic dynamics. It forms the basis for financial mathematics, such as calculating the present value of an income stream.

Semester II

Core Course 3: Introductory Macroeconomics

This course provides an overview of the economy as a whole, a core part of the first-year economics curriculum. It introduces students to the measurement of macroeconomic variables like GDP, inflation, and unemployment. It also covers the classical and Keynesian models of income determination and the role of fiscal and monetary policy.

Key Topics:

1. Introduction to Macroeconomics and National Income Accounting

This unit introduces the key concerns of macroeconomics, such as growth and business cycles. You will learn how economists measure national output and income using Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and its various components.

2. The Labour Market and Unemployment

This topic explores the concepts of the labor force, employment, and unemployment. You will learn about different types of unemployment (frictional, structural, cyclical) and how wages are determined in the classical model.

3. Money, Inflation, and the Financial System

This unit defines what money is and the role of the central bank (like the RBI) and commercial banks in creating it. It also introduces the Quantity Theory of Money to explain the long-run relationship between money supply and inflation.

4. The Closed Economy in the Short Run (IS-LM Model)

This is a central model for short-run analysis. You will learn how the goods market (IS curve) and the money market (LM curve) interact to determine the economy's equilibrium interest rate and output level.

5. Fiscal and Monetary Policy

Using the IS-LM framework, this topic analyzes the power of government policy. You'll study how government spending (fiscal policy) and changes in the money supply (monetary policy) can be used to influence economic activity and stabilize the economy.

Core Course 4: Mathematical Methods for Economics-II

Building on the first semester, this course introduces multi-variable calculus and linear algebra. These tools are crucial for understanding advanced economic theories, including general equilibrium and optimization problems with multiple constraints, and are a vital part of the mathematical methods for economics.

Key Topics:

1. Functions of Several Real Variables

This unit extends the concept of a function to include multiple inputs, such as a production function that depends on both labor and capital. You will learn about partial derivatives, which measure the impact of changing one variable while holding others constant.

2. Multi-variable Optimization (Unconstrained and Constrained)

This is a critical tool for all of economics. You will learn the Lagrangian method to solve constrained optimization problems, such as a consumer maximizing utility subject to a budget constraint or a firm minimizing costs for a given output level.

3. Linear Algebra: Vectors and Matrices

This topic introduces matrices and vectors, which are essential for organizing and solving large systems of equations. This is the foundation for advanced econometrics and general equilibrium models.

4. Systems of Linear Equations

Using matrix algebra, you will learn systematic methods (like Cramer's Rule) to solve systems of linear equations. This is directly applicable to finding the equilibrium in models with multiple markets or sectors.

5. Integration and Differential Equations

This unit covers integration (the reverse of differentiation), which is used to find total values from marginal ones (e.g., finding total cost from marginal cost). Differential equations are introduced to model how economic variables change over time.
