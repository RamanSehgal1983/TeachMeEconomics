Third Year Economics Curriculum

Advanced topics in applied and development economics from the third-year economics honors syllabus. This final year transitions from pure theory to practical application, focusing on some of the most pressing and complex real-world economic issues. You will apply the theoretical and econometric tools you've mastered to analyze the specific case of the Indian economy, examining its historical development and contemporary policy debates. Alongside this, you will explore the multifaceted field of Development Economics, studying the causes of poverty and the strategies for promoting growth and well-being globally.

Semester V

Core Course 11: Indian Economy-I

This course provides a comprehensive overview of the Indian economy's key features and policies, particularly focusing on the period after independence. It examines the role of different sectors and analyzes trends in national income, savings, and investment, a crucial part of the Indian Economy paper.

Key Topics:

1. Economic Development since Independence

This topic reviews the state of the Indian economy at independence and traces its evolution. It covers the "Hindu rate of growth" debate and analyzes the broad structural changes in the economy, particularly the shift away from agriculture in terms of national income.

2. Population and Human Development

This unit explores India's demographic transition, analyzing trends in population growth, density, and the "demographic dividend." It also assesses India's progress on key human development indicators such as health, education, and gender equity.

3. Growth and Distribution Trends

This topic examines the trends in national income, savings, and investment that have driven India's growth story. It also critically analyzes the distributive outcomes, focusing on the persistent challenges of poverty and inequality across different regions and social groups.

4. Policies and Performance in Agriculture

This unit focuses on the agricultural sector's performance, starting with the land reform initiatives after independence. It then analyzes the causes and consequences of the Green Revolution, including its impact on productivity, rural wages, and regional disparities.

5. The Role of the Public and Private Sectors

This topic examines the mixed economy model adopted by India. It covers the rationale for a large public sector, the Industrial Policy Resolutions, and the 'License Raj,' critically evaluating the performance and inefficiencies that led to subsequent reforms.

Core Course 12: Development Economics-I

This course introduces students to the study of development economics. It covers the main theories of development and underdevelopment, analyzes the sources of economic growth, and examines issues of poverty, inequality, and population growth in developing countries.

Key Topics:

1. Conceptions of Development

This unit goes beyond simple income measures to explore multidimensional concepts of development, such as Amartya Sen's "capability approach." It also introduces key metrics like the Human Development Index (HDI) and discusses the characteristics of underdevelopment.

2. Growth Models and Empirics

This topic introduces foundational models of economic growth, such as the Harrod-Domar model and the Lewis model of structural change. It provides a theoretical framework for understanding how countries accumulate capital and transition from agrarian to industrial economies.

3. Poverty and Inequality: Definitions and Measurement

This unit focuses on the critical challenges of poverty and inequality. You will learn various ways to measure them, such as the poverty headcount ratio and the Gini coefficient, and explore concepts like the "poverty trap," where poverty becomes self-perpetuating.

4. Political Institutions and the Functioning of the State

This topic explores the crucial role of institutions in economic development. It examines how factors like property rights, the rule of law, and the level of corruption can either promote or hinder economic growth and equitable development.

5. Population Growth and its Economic Implications

This unit analyzes the relationship between population growth and economic development. You will study the theory of demographic transition and explore the economic consequences of changing population structures, such as aging populations or "youth bulges."

Semester VI

Core Course 13: Indian Economy-II

This course focuses on the macroeconomic policies and sectoral performance of the Indian economy, especially in the post-1991 reform era. It covers India's external sector, fiscal policy, monetary policy, and key debates on economic reforms, providing an advanced understanding of the Indian economy.

Key Topics:

1. Macroeconomic Policies and Their Impact

This unit analyzes the conduct of fiscal and monetary policy in India, especially after the 1991 reforms. It covers topics like fiscal consolidation, the FRBM Act, inflation targeting, and the role of the Monetary Policy Committee.

2. Policies and Performance in Industry and Services

This topic reviews the deregulation of the industrial sector ("delicensing") and the rise of the services sector as the main driver of India's growth. It examines policies related to competition, privatization, and foreign direct investment (FDI).

3. India's Foreign Trade and Balance of Payments

This unit covers the shift from an inward-looking (import substitution) to an outward-looking (export promotion) trade strategy. You will analyze India's balance of payments, currency convertibility, and the evolution of its exchange rate policy.

4. Fiscal Federalism and Centre-State Financial Relations

This topic explores the financial relationship between the central government and state governments in India. It covers the role of the Finance Commission in tax sharing and the recent shift in intergovernmental transfers, including the impact of the Goods and Services Tax (GST).

5. Contemporary Economic Reforms and Debates

This unit engages with current policy debates in India. This may include topics such as the challenges in the banking sector (NPAs), agricultural distress, the formalization of the economy, and the debates surrounding privatization and labor law reforms.

Core Course 14: Development Economics-II

This course delves into specific topics in development economics, focusing on the role of various markets and institutions. It examines issues related to land, labor, and credit markets, as well as the importance of environment, trade, and globalization for development. This is a key course in applied economics.

Key Topics:

1. Land, Labor, and Credit Markets in Developing Countries

This topic analyzes the imperfections in key factor markets in developing countries. It explores issues like sharecropping in land markets, dualism in labor markets (formal vs. informal), and the challenges of access to credit for the poor.

2. Environment and Sustainable Development

This unit explores the critical link between economic development and the environment. It introduces the concept of sustainable development and analyzes policy tools to manage environmental resources, addressing the "tragedy of the commons."

3. International Trade and Development Strategy

This topic examines the role of international trade in the development process. It contrasts inward-looking (Import Substitution) and outward-looking (Export Promotion) strategies and analyzes the arguments for and against trade liberalization.

4. Globalization and its Impact on Developing Economies

This unit assesses the broader impact of globalization, including foreign direct investment (FDI) and the role of multinational corporations (MNCs). It discusses the opportunities and challenges that globalization presents for developing nations.

5. Microfinance and its Role in Poverty Alleviation

This topic provides a micro-level view of development policy. It critically examines the rise of microfinance and group lending (like the Grameen Bank model) as a tool to alleviate poverty by providing credit access to the poor, and evaluates its effectiveness.
