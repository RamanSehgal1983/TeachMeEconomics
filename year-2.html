Second Year Economics Curriculum

Diving deeper into economic theory and statistical analysis with the second-year economics honors syllabus. This intermediate year marks a significant step up in analytical rigor, moving beyond introductory concepts to explore more complex and formal economic models. You will build a robust understanding of both micro and macroeconomic theory, which forms the core of modern economic analysis. Furthermore, this year introduces you to the essential tools of empirical research, teaching you how to apply statistical methods and econometrics to test theories against real-world data.

Semester III

Core Course 5: Intermediate Microeconomics-I

This course provides a rigorous treatment of consumer and producer theory, a cornerstone of intermediate microeconomics. It analyzes how consumers make choices to maximize utility and how firms make decisions to maximize profits, covering concepts like budget constraints, indifference curves, and cost minimization.

Key Topics:

1. Consumer Theory: Preferences, Budget Constraints, and Choice

This unit formally models consumer behavior using indifference curves (representing preferences) and budget lines (representing constraints). You will learn to graphically and mathematically derive the optimal consumption bundle that maximizes a consumer's utility.

2. Demand Functions and Comparative Statics

Building on consumer theory, this topic explores how the optimal choice changes when income or prices change. It introduces the Slutsky equation to decompose the effect of a price change into the income effect and the substitution effect.

3. Producer Theory: Technology, Cost Minimization, and Profit Maximization

This unit models the firm's decision-making process. You will use isoquants and isocost lines to find the least-cost combination of inputs (like labor and capital) needed to produce a given level of output and analyze how firms maximize profits.

4. Supply Functions and Market Equilibrium

This topic derives the firm's supply curve from its cost structure. It then combines the market supply curve (sum of all firms) with the market demand curve (sum of all consumers) to analyze the determination of price and quantity in a perfectly competitive market.

Core Course 6: Intermediate Macroeconomics-I

This intermediate macroeconomics course delves into aggregate demand and supply. It formally derives the IS-LM model for a closed economy and extends it to an open economy using the Mundell-Fleming model. The course analyzes the effects of fiscal and monetary policy under different exchange rate regimes.

Key Topics:

1. Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

This topic introduces the primary model used to explain short-run economic fluctuations. You will learn to derive the Aggregate Demand (AD) and Aggregate Supply (AS) curves and use them to analyze the effects of shocks and policies on output and the price level.

2. The IS-LM Model in Detail

This unit provides a deep dive into the IS (Investment-Saving) and LM (Liquidity-Money) model. You will formally derive both curves and use the framework to analyze the short-run effectiveness of fiscal policy (government spending, taxes) and monetary policy (money supply).

3. The Open Economy: Mundell-Fleming Model

This topic extends the IS-LM model to an open economy by adding a Balance of Payments (BP) curve. This powerful model allows for the analysis of economies that trade goods and assets with the rest of the world, incorporating exchange rates and capital flows.

4. Policy Effects with Fixed and Flexible Exchange Rates

Using the Mundell-Fleming model, you will compare the effectiveness of fiscal and monetary policy under two different systems. You'll discover why monetary policy is powerful with flexible exchange rates, while fiscal policy is more effective with fixed exchange rates.

Core Course 7: Statistical Methods for Economics

This course introduces the statistical methods necessary for empirical economic research and econometrics. It covers probability theory, sampling, estimation, and hypothesis testing, laying the groundwork for the study of econometrics.

Key Topics:

1. Probability Theory and Distributions

This foundational unit introduces the concepts of probability, random variables, and their expected values. You will study key probability distributions that are essential for statistics, such as the Binomial, Poisson, and Normal distributions.

2. Sampling and Point/Interval Estimation

This topic explores how to learn about a large population from a small sample. You will learn the properties of good estimators (like unbiasedness) and how to construct confidence intervals (e.g., "we are 95% confident the mean is between X and Y").

3. Hypothesis Testing for Means and Proportions

This unit provides a formal framework for testing economic claims. You will learn to set up a null and alternative hypothesis (e.g., "this policy had no effect") and use sample data to determine whether to reject the claim, using concepts like p-values and t-statistics.

4. Correlation and Introduction to Simple Regression

This topic introduces methods for analyzing the relationship between two variables. You will learn to measure the strength of a linear relationship (correlation) and begin to model it using simple linear regression, which forms the basis of econometrics.

Semester IV

Core Course 8: Intermediate Microeconomics-II

This course focuses on general equilibrium, welfare economics, and market failures. It explores how different markets interact, the conditions for economic efficiency, and situations where markets fail to produce optimal outcomes, such as in the presence of externalities and public goods.

Key Topics:

1. General Equilibrium and Efficiency

This unit moves beyond analyzing a single market (partial equilibrium) to studying all markets simultaneously (general equilibrium). Using the Edgeworth Box, you will analyze trade between individuals to find a Pareto efficient allocation, where no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off.

2. Welfare Economics and the Fundamental Theorems

This topic explores the connection between competitive markets and social welfare. You will study the First Fundamental Theorem of Welfare (which states that competitive equilibria are efficient) and the Second Welfare Theorem (which deals with achieving fairness).

3. Market Failure: Externalities and Public Goods

This unit analyzes why markets sometimes fail to produce an efficient outcome. You will study externalities (like pollution), where actions have unintended side effects, and public goods (like national defense), which are non-rival and non-excludable.

4. Asymmetric Information (Adverse Selection and Moral Hazard)

This topic explores problems that arise when one party in a transaction has more information than the other. You will learn about adverse selection (e.g., the market for "lemons" or used cars) and moral hazard (e.g., how insurance can change behavior).

Core Course 9: Intermediate Macroeconomics-II

This course examines the microfoundations of macroeconomics and explores key macroeconomic issues like inflation, unemployment, and economic growth. It introduces models such as the Phillips Curve and the Solow Growth Model to analyze these phenomena.

Key Topics:

1. Microfoundations of Consumption and Investment

This unit explores the theories behind the aggregate consumption and investment functions. You will study models like Keynes's conjecture, the Life-Cycle Hypothesis, and the Permanent Income Hypothesis to understand what drives long-term consumption and saving.

2. The Phillips Curve and the Inflation-Unemployment Tradeoff

This topic analyzes the short-run tradeoff between inflation and unemployment, known as the Phillips Curve. You will learn why this relationship breaks down in the long run and explore the role of expectations and the concept of the "natural rate of unemployment."

3. Economic Growth: The Solow Model

This unit introduces the Solow-Swan model, the most famous model of long-run economic growth. You will use it to understand how capital accumulation, population growth, and technological progress determine a country's standard of living and long-run growth rate.

4. Business Cycles and Macroeconomic Policy Debates

This topic explores theories of short-run economic fluctuations (business cycles). It also covers major debates in macroeconomics, such as whether policymakers should follow fixed rules or use their discretion to stabilize the economy.

Core Course 10: Introductory Econometrics

This course provides a comprehensive introduction to econometrics. It focuses on the theory and application of the classical linear regression model, teaching students how to estimate economic relationships using real-world data and how to deal with common problems that arise in empirical work. This is a foundational course for anyone interested in applied economics.

Key Topics:

1. The Classical Linear Regression Model (CLRM)

This unit introduces the foundational model for econometric analysis. You will learn the key assumptions of the CLRM, which are necessary for our estimation techniques to have desirable properties.

2. Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Estimation

This topic covers the most common method for estimating the parameters of a regression model. You will learn how OLS works (minimizing the sum of squared residuals) and how to interpret the resulting coefficients (e.g., "a one-unit increase in X is associated with a Y-unit increase in Z").

3. Hypothesis Testing and Model Specification

This unit teaches you how to test the statistical significance of your results using t-tests and F-tests. You will also learn how to choose the correct variables and functional form for your model to avoid specification errors.

4. Violations of Classical Assumptions (e.g., Heteroskedasticity)

This topic explores what happens when the CLRM assumptions are violated, a common problem in real-world data. You will learn to detect and correct for issues like heteroskedasticity (non-constant variance) and multicollinearity (highly correlated independent variables).

5. Dummy Variables and Qualitative Regressors

This unit introduces a powerful tool for incorporating categorical information into your models. You will learn to use dummy (binary) variables to capture the effects of factors like gender, location, or the impact of a policy change.
