Core Course 12: Development Economics-I

This course introduces the core concepts and theories of economic development, with a focus on growth, poverty, and inequality.

Course Content Details

1. Conceptions of Development

This introductory unit explores the multifaceted nature of development. It moves beyond narrow definitions based purely on income growth (like GDP per capita) to embrace broader concepts. A key focus is Amartya Sen's Capability Approach, which defines development as the expansion of human freedoms and capabilities. We will also examine various composite indices used to measure development, such as the Human Development Index (HDI), and discuss the characteristics common to developing countries.

Key Topics:

  • Development as Growth vs. Development as Freedom (Sen's Capability Approach)
  • Measuring Development: Human Development Index (HDI) and other indices
  • Common Characteristics of Developing Economies
  • The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

2. Grand Theories of Development and Underdevelopment

This unit surveys the major "grand theories" that attempted to explain economic development and the persistent gap between rich and poor nations. We will cover the linear stages of growth models (like Rostow's), structural change models (like Lewis's two-sector model), and the international dependence theories that emphasize the role of the global power structure. These theories provide historical context for contemporary development debates.

Key Topics:

  • Rostow's Stages of Growth
  • The Harrod-Domar Growth Model
  • The Lewis Two-Sector Model of Structural Change
  • Dependency Theory and the Center-Periphery Model

3. Poverty and Inequality: Definitions, Measures, and Mechanisms

This unit focuses on two of the most critical challenges in development. We will learn how to define and measure poverty (e.g., poverty lines, headcount ratio) and inequality (e.g., Lorenz curve, Gini coefficient). The unit then explores the mechanisms that perpetuate inequality, such as unequal access to credit and education. A key concept is the Kuznets' inverted-U hypothesis, which posits a relationship between inequality and the level of development.

Key Topics:

  • Measuring Absolute and Relative Poverty
  • The Lorenz Curve and the Gini Coefficient
  • The Kuznets' Inverted-U Hypothesis
  • Poverty Traps and the Role of Credit Constraints

4. Political Institutions and the Functioning of the State

This unit examines the crucial role of institutions and governance in the development process. It explores how different political systems and the quality of state institutions can either foster or hinder economic progress. Topics include the problem of corruption, the importance of property rights, and the debate around different models of governance. The focus is on understanding that good policies are ineffective without capable and accountable institutions to implement them.

Key Topics:

  • The Role of the State in Economic Development
  • Corruption and its Economic Consequences
  • The Importance of Secure Property Rights
  • Democracy vs. Autocracy in Development
